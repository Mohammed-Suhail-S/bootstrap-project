WASP-121b


In English


WASP-121b, also known as a "hot Jupiter," is an extraordinary exoplanet located approximately 880 light-years away from Earth in the constellation Puppis. Discovered in 2015 by the Wide Angle Search for Planets (WASP) project, this gas giant orbits its parent star, WASP-121, at an exceptionally close distance of just 0.025 astronomical units (AU). Due to its extreme proximity to its host star, WASP-121b experiences extraordinarily high temperatures, with its dayside atmosphere heating up to around 2,500°C (4,532°F). This intense heat causes the planet's upper atmosphere to expand and escape into space, a phenomenon observed in only a few exoplanets. The planet completes an orbit around its star in just about 1.3 Earth days, making it one of the shortest orbital periods known for any exoplanet.


WASP-121b has a mass approximately 1.18 times that of Jupiter and a radius about 1.81 times greater, making it significantly larger and more inflated than Jupiter. This inflation is due to the extreme heat, which causes the planet's atmosphere to swell. The planet's atmosphere is rich in exotic compounds, including vanadium oxide and titanium oxide, which are typically found in the atmospheres of cool stars rather than planets. These compounds contribute to the planet's unique and dynamic atmospheric properties, including the presence of a stratosphere where temperatures increase with altitude, akin to Earth's atmosphere.


The discovery of WASP-121b has provided valuable insights into the atmospheric dynamics and chemistry of hot Jupiters. Its proximity to its parent star allows for detailed studies of how intense stellar radiation affects planetary atmospheres. Observations with instruments like the Hubble Space Telescope and the upcoming James Webb Space Telescope have revealed the presence of water vapor, exotic clouds, and evidence of atmospheric escape, where the outer layers of the planet's atmosphere are being stripped away by the star's radiation. These findings are crucial for understanding the processes that govern atmospheric retention and loss in exoplanets.


WASP-121b's extreme conditions make it an ideal candidate for studying the physical and chemical properties of hot Jupiters. The planet's inflated atmosphere and close orbit provide a natural laboratory for examining the effects of stellar radiation on planetary environments. The insights gained from studying WASP-121b help refine models of planetary formation and evolution, particularly for gas giants in extreme environments. Additionally, the study of WASP-121b's atmosphere offers a glimpse into the potential diversity of planetary atmospheres and the complex interplay between planets and their host stars.


As observational techniques and technologies advance, WASP-121b remains a key target for exoplanet research. Its unique characteristics offer a wealth of information about the mechanisms that shape planetary atmospheres and the potential for exotic weather patterns and chemical compositions. The ongoing exploration of WASP-121b and similar exoplanets not only enhances our understanding of gas giant planets but also contributes to the broader field of exoplanetary science, revealing the diverse and dynamic nature of planets beyond our solar system.
